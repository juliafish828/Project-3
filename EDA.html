EDA for Diabetes Health Indicators Information - 2015

Introduction

Over the course of the past few decades, diabetes prevalence in everyday life has sky-rocketed. With many lifestyle aspects changing alongside this disease’s relevance, it becomes extremely important to look into several factors to discern potential patterns and relationships between diabetes diagnoses and other medical and lifestyle information. We will do part of this exploration here.

This project uses the Diabetes Health Indicators BRFSS 2015 dataset from Kaggle to explore indicators potentially related to diabetes status. This dataset includes information on lifestyle, personal demographics, and medical history from a large sample of people.

This data was collected using The Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System, a CDC annual telephone survey (Teboul 2021). This data gets placed into 3 data sets for increased organization, usability, and potential for analysis.

This EDA will focus on three predictors in particular as well as the response:

  • BMI (Body Mass Index): a medically defined value to assess potential obesity using height and weight (high levels of body fat is commonly associated with diabetes)
  • gen_hlth (General Health): a categorical variable describing the general health rating one gives themselves (lower levels of general health could be related to prevelance/risk of diabetes)
  • HighBP (High Blood Pressure): a binary variable indicating whether or not the subject has high blood pressure (a common comorbidity with diabetes)

As stated above, we will also look at the response variable, diabetes_binary, which indicates the presence of diabetes (or lack thereof).

Our goal with this exploratory data analysis is to identify potential relationships in predictors and response. That will help us gain initial information on how the data works as well as be useful in further analysis to predict whether an individual has diabetes given these predictors, which is the ultimate goal of this project (i.e. the modeling portion).

Bivariate Analysis

We will start with analysis of BMI with diabetes.

## BMI vs Diabetes
ggplot(data, aes(x = diabetes_binary, y = bmi, fill = diabetes_binary)) +
  geom_boxplot() +
  labs(title = "BMI by Diabetes Status",
       x = "Diabetes Status", y = "BMI") +
  scale_fill_manual(values = c("No_Diabetes" = "lightblue", "Diabetes" = "pink")) +
  theme(legend.position = "none")

It can be seen from the plot above that the spread of BMI is not much different for those with a diabetes diagnosis versus those without. The median as well as Q1 and Q3 values are all higher for those with diabetes, but not by much. Let’s look at a table with each 5 number summary to decipher these exact differences:

summary_table <- data %>%
  group_by(diabetes_binary) %>%
  summarise(
    Min = min(bmi, na.rm = TRUE),
    Q1 = quantile(bmi, 0.25, na.rm = TRUE),
    Median = median(bmi, na.rm = TRUE),
    Q3 = quantile(bmi, 0.75, na.rm = TRUE),
    Max = max(bmi, na.rm = TRUE)
  )

print(summary_table)

We will now move on to analyze high blood pressure and diabetes together.

## High BP vs Diabetes
ggplot(data, aes(x = high_bp, fill = diabetes_binary)) +

The plot above is another visual representation of the low percentage of people in this data set with a diabetes diagnosis (i.e. not a lot of pink compared to the blue). It is important to note, however, that there is a lot higher of a percentage of people with a diabetes diagnosis that also have high blood pressure than those who have diabetes and not high blood pressure. We will explore this numerically with a contingency table.

prop.table(table(data$high_bp, data$diabetes_binary), margin = 1)
            
             No_Diabetes   Diabetes
  No_High_BP  0.93964833 0.06035167
  High_BP     0.75554310 0.24445690

This shows that large difference in those with a diabetes diagnosis once again. In addition, the lower diagonal has more values than the other diagonal, showing once again that having a high blood pressure diagnosis seemingly impacts the risk/prevalence of diabetes and vice versa. This potential connection is great to know before moving to model training, selection, and fitting.

This plot is extremely helpful in determining information on how these variables intertwine. As stated earlier during univariate analysis, it was challenging to have impactful takeaways on just general health rating. This plot, however, shows that diabetes monotonically increases as the general health rating gets lower (categorically lower). This confirms that there could possibly be an inverse relationship between these variables, which could point to general health rating being a good predictor for predicting diabetes status using this data.
